Maps, Location, and Cartographers

Maps of Norway

Modern Political Map (Mercator Projection)

This modern political map of Norway uses the Mercator projection, which preserves direction but distorts size near the poles. It shows major cities like Oslo, Bergen, and Trondheim, along with national borders and coastline.

Location and Latitude

Norway is located in Northern Europe on the Scandinavian Peninsula. Its latitudinal range is approximately 58°N to 71°N, placing much of the country in high-latitude regions.

Viking Trade Routes

During the Viking Age (800–1100 CE), Norse sailors from Norway traveled across Europe and the North Atlantic. These routes connected Norway to England, Iceland, and beyond.

Physical and Topographic Map

This map shows Norway's rugged terrain, including mountains and fjords formed by glaciers. The landscape explains why settlements are concentrated along the coast.

More About Norway

📍

Where is Norway located?

Norway is located on the continent of Europe, in the northern part of the Scandinavian Peninsula in Northern Europe. It is bordered by Sweden to the east, Finland and Russia to the northeast, and faces the North Sea, the Norwegian Sea, and the Barents Sea to the west and north.

🌐

What is the latitudinal range?

Norway stretches from approximately 57°N to 71°N latitude, spanning roughly 14 degrees of latitude. This places the country largely within the subarctic and Arctic climate zones, resulting in diverse climatic conditions from temperate oceanic in the south to polar in the far north.

🕰️

When did Norway exist as a country?

The Kingdom of Norway has existed as a unified kingdom since approximately 872 AD under Harald Fairhair. It was part of the Kalmar Union (1397–1523), a union with Denmark (1523–1814), a brief independent period, a union with Sweden (1814–1905), and has been a sovereign constitutional monarchy since 1905.

🧭

Important cartographers

Peder Claussøn Friis (1545–1614) was one of Norway's earliest cartographers, producing detailed written descriptions and maps of Norway in his work Norriges og omliggende Øers sandfærdige Bescriffuelse (1632). From other countries, Gerardus Mercator (1512–1594) developed the Mercator projection in 1569, which became the standard for nautical charts of Norway and the world. Abraham Ortelius (1527–1598) included early printed maps of Scandinavia in his landmark atlas Theatrum Orbis Terrarum (1570). The Norwegian Mapping Authority (Statens kartverk), established in 1773, undertook systematic national mapping that remains foundational.

Boundary Changes Over Time

Norway's boundaries have remained relatively stable since the dissolution of the union with Sweden in 1905. However, the map shows the historical expansion under Charles XII (1697-1721) when Norway controlled territories including Jämtland and parts of modern Sweden. The modern borders established in 1920 have remained consistent, though the Svalbard archipelago remains under Norwegian sovereignty (Bjørkøe, 2014).
